1. Norman’s Model (Execution–Evaluation Cycle)

Norman’s model explains interaction as a continuous cycle between the user’s mental goals and the system’s visible state. The model is primarily used to identify usability breakdowns caused by poor mappings, feedback, or system visibility (Dix et al., Ch. 3).

Seven Stages of Action – Illustrated Using a Light Switch

  1. Goal: The user decides that additional light is needed.
  2. Intention: The user plans to turn on the light.
  3. Specification: The user determines the physical actions required.
  4. Execution: The switch is pressed.
  5. Perception: The user observes the room becoming brighter.
  6. Interpretation: The brightness is understood as the light being on.
  7. Evaluation: The user confirms that the goal has been achieved.

Design Insight: Problems often occur when users cannot easily discover how to perform actions or cannot clearly interpret system feedback.

Limitations: The model does not differentiate clearly between novice and expert behavior and focuses more on understanding interaction than predicting speed.

Reference: Dix et al., Human–Computer Interaction, Chapter 3.

2. GOMS Model

GOMS is an analytical framework used to describe how experienced users perform tasks efficiently. Unlike Norman’s model, GOMS focuses on task structure rather than feedback interpretation (Dix et al., Ch. 9).

  • Goals: Desired outcomes the user wants to achieve.
  • Operators: Elementary actions such as keystrokes or mouse clicks.
  • Methods: Ordered sequences of operators used to complete a goal.
  • Selection Rules: Conditions that determine which method is chosen.

Expert User Assumption: GOMS assumes users already understand the interface, so perception and evaluation stages are not modeled explicitly.

Example: Expense Entry Task (Who Paid Scenario)

GOAL: RECORD-EXPENSE
.   [METHOD: QUICK-ENTRY
.           SELECT-ADD-EXPENSE
.           ENTER-NAMES
.           ENTER-AMOUNT
.           CONFIRM-SAVE
.
.    METHOD: GROUP-BASED-ENTRY
.           SELECT-GROUP
.           ADD-EXPENSE-TO-GROUP
.           ENTER-AMOUNT
.           CONFIRM-SAVE]

Such hierarchical task descriptions help designers evaluate cognitive workload, predict efficiency, and compare alternative interface designs.

Reference: Dix et al., Human–Computer Interaction, Chapter 9.

3. Keystroke-Level Model (KLM)

The Keystroke-Level Model is a quantitative extension of GOMS that predicts how long it takes an expert user to perform routine tasks without errors. It is most suitable for well-learned, repetitive actions (Dix et al., Ch. 12).

KLM Focus Areas

  • Mental Preparation: Cognitive readiness before action sequences.
  • Physical Execution: Keystrokes, pointing, clicking, and hand movement.

References: Dix, A., Finlay, J., Abowd, G., Beale, R. Human–Computer Interaction, 3rd Edition. Pearson Education, 2004. Chapters 3, 9, and 12.
